Research

Ecological communities are the product of multiple ecological (e.g., competition or environmental filtering) and evolutionary drivers (e.g., speciation and extinction; niche conservatism and limiting similarity). Because local species assembly is both a product and driver of regional patterns, ecologists are increasingly interested in eco-evolutionary patterns at and across different spatial scales.

Phylogenetic patterns at fine-spatial scales

Rouge Valley Community ecologists tend to focus on patterns at the community-level scale to explain processes like competitive exclusion or environmental filtering. However, the spatial context may be incongruous to the process of interest. For instance, while biotic interactions occur between individuals, they are instead often measured at the community scale.

With plant community data collected from the Rouge Valley Urban National Park in Scarborough, ON, I am interested in how phylogenetic and trait patterns shift at fine-spatial scales; and moreover the spatial scales that appropriately capture phylogenetic and trait patterns.

Eco-evolutionary patterns in Range Size

Local community and regional assembly patterns are intricately linked. One way to reconcile local and regional assembly patterns is by exploring how a species’ regional expanse might inform us of broad-scale assembly processes act on its distribution, but also how the species might interact locally. By combining species' range size with the evolutionary histories between species, I explore how evolution influences species coexistence at both local and broad spatial scales.

With data collected by the NPS and USGS for plant communities in the Rocky Mountain National Park, CO, I specifically ask if there are obvious evolutionary patterns in the size of species’ ranges. Or in other words, whether the size of a species' range predicts how closely it is related to its neighbors a community.

Phylogenetic Betadiversity Assessment

Beta-diversity helps us understand how ecological communities change across gradients. Phylogenetic beta-diversity ("phylobetadiversity") contributes information on how species lineages shift across space, but moreover the phylogenetic depth at which changes across space occur. I am interested in the novel contribution of phylobetadiversity metrics, and how they may complement taxonomic metrics in informing us about the eco-evolutionary drivers behind species coexistence patterns.

With phylobetadiversity, I analyze the role of environment and space in shaping patterns of phylogenetic turnover among communities. For angiosperm plant communities in the Rocky Mountain National Park, CO, I have found patterns consistent with niche conservatism.

Jin L.S., M.W. Cadotte, M.-J. Fortin. 2015. Phylogenetic turnover patterns consistent with niche conservatism in montane plant species. Journal of Ecology, 103: 742-749.

Phylogenetic Diversity Metric Behavior

The present literature is proliferate with phylogenetic metrics intended to address different questions across disparate biological fields. The sPhy working group conceptually linked the existing body of phylogenetic metrics from three broad biological disciplines: community ecology, macroecology and conservation biology.

Different eco-evolutionary processes may however affect how the metrics behave. For instance, the strength of trait conservation, the shape of the phylogenetic tree, or size of the species pool may all influence species interactions in a landscape. With simulated data under the SCAPE landscape framework, I explore how different factors influence the behavior of phylodiversity metrics meant to capture species coexistence patterns in a landscape.

Project Name

The "Comprehensive Guide to Phylogeny Building" is a guide to building molecular phylogenetic trees as intended for data analysis in community ecology. While this guide provides the slow and laborious process of tree building compared to new quick and dirty methods (e.g., phyloGenerator), it nonetheless provides detailed methodology to create a robust accurate tree.
